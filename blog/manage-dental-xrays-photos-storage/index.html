How to organise dental clinical files without wasting storage

Clinical files are useful only when they can be found quickly, linked to the correct patient and stored at a sensible size.

A phone gallery can hold thousands of dental images and still function as a poor clinical record because nobody can reliably find the correct patient later.

Every file needs a patient and a purpose

Before uploading, confirm the patient, date and reason for the file. A useful label may identify whether it is a pre-operative X-ray, post-operative X-ray, prescription, lab report or progress photograph.

Do not save clinical files only under the date they were taken. Dates help, but the patient relationship is what makes the file retrievable.

Separate file types clearly

  • X-rays: diagnostic and treatment-stage images.
  • Prescriptions: medication documents issued or received.
  • Reports: lab, pathology or referral documents.
  • Clinical photos: before, during and after treatment.
  • Administrative files: consent or insurance documents where applicable.

Consistent categories make patient history easier to scan and reduce accidental uploads to the wrong section.

Keep enough quality, not unnecessary size

A clinical image must remain readable. Compression should reduce excess file size without making important detail unusable. Avoid repeated screenshots, social-media exports and heavily edited copies when the original is available.

Practical rule

Keep the clearest useful version. Delete blurred, accidental and duplicate files before upload. Use smaller copies for routine viewing, but preserve the original when clinical detail requires it.

Prevent duplicate uploads

Duplicates usually appear when the same file is uploaded by reception and the doctor, or when a patient sends the image again through another channel. Before uploading, check whether the file already exists in the patient gallery.

A monthly review of the largest files can recover storage without deleting useful clinical history.

Control who can see and upload files

Clinical files should follow the same clinic-based access rules as patient records. Staff should use individual accounts rather than shared passwords, and files should not be moved through personal devices unless clinic policy allows it.

When a staff member leaves, remove their access without affecting the clinic’s files.

What to do when storage reaches its limit

Do not delete useful patient history merely to make room. First remove duplicates, accidental captures and files saved at unnecessarily large dimensions. Then consider additional storage based on the clinic’s real use of X-rays, photographs and reports.

CapDent includes 1 GB of cloud storage. Existing files should remain part of the patient record; additional uploads can be managed through storage upgrades when they become available.

Clinical-file checklist

  • Confirm the patient before every upload.
  • Use a clear file category.
  • Keep the date and clinical purpose visible.
  • Remove blurred and accidental images.
  • Check for duplicates.
  • Use individual staff accounts.
  • Review storage use monthly.

Keep clinical files with the patient—not scattered across devices.
